RiftLand 2.0

By the GMU MURI Modeling Team (alphabetically by first names): Ates, Bill, Habib, Joey, Siggy, & Tim. Alumni include Chenna, Gabrial, Jeff, Mark C., Mark R., & Meghan.

This is an Agent-Based Model (ABM) of a 1,000 mile square region encompassing Lake Victoria and the Rift Valley of Eastern Africa. This model represents the environment, people, conflict, and migration in the region. The area spans the equator and is bounded by latitudes: 7.81133North and 5.7719979South and longitudes: 28.264East and 42.38066102East.

The environment is represented as 1,600 by 1,600 1km square parcels based on GIS data, with grazable land, urban areas, lakes, rivers, and some of the Indian Ocean. The weather is based on data collected every 16 days for 30km squares covering the whole area.

RiftLand 2.0 (May 2013) Runnable version with no functionality bugs post ONR review.

RiftLand 1.8 (May 2013) optimized to run full region at full population and now includes a programmable disaster.

RiftLand 1.5 (Aug 2012) supported initialization of full population densities.

RiftLand 1.3 (July 2012) included movement of displaced rural people moving to cities in search of aid.

The next version 1.1, (July 2012) incorporated change to integrate activities agents into one agent per household.

RiftLand 1.2 (July 2012) incorporated a second farming model, this one for high intensity farming.

RiftLand 1.0, the third version (May 2012), incorporated household decision-making among activities (agents) used to make a living and creates displaced people. The displaced population is moved to cities and camps along the road network. This version was announced with the ONR program review.

RiftLand 0.2, the second version 0.2 (5 July 2011) was a redesign of the agents to have rural households with activities used to make a living (farming, herding, and labor) with plans to model urban households and IDPs as urban-level agents.

RiftLand 0.1, the first version (13 July 2010), was based on applying the HerderLand model to the Rift Valley.
